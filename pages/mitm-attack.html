Man-in-the-Middle Attack

In unauthenticated DH, Alice and Bob have no way to verify who they're exchanging keys with. Eve exploits this by intercepting both public keys and substituting her own — creating two separate shared secrets while Alice and Bob believe they share one secure channel.

Message Interception Flow

Step-by-Step Breakdown

1

Setup: Eve Positions Herself

Eve controls the network path between Alice and Bob (e.g., a compromised router). She can intercept, drop, and inject any packet. Alice and Bob do not know Eve exists.

2

Alice Sends Y_A — Eve Intercepts

Alice sends her DH public key Y_A intended for Bob. Eve intercepts it silently. Bob never receives Alice's real key.

3

Eve Substitutes Her Own Key to Bob

Eve generates her own key pair and sends her public key Y_E to Bob, pretending to be Alice. Bob computes a shared secret with Eve, thinking it's with Alice.

4

Bob Sends Y_B — Eve Intercepts Again

Bob sends his public key to Alice. Eve intercepts it and substitutes her own key, so Alice computes a shared secret with Eve, thinking it's with Bob.

5

Two Separate Secrets — Eve Controls Both

K1 = shared between Alice and Eve. K2 = shared between Bob and Eve. Eve can decrypt messages from Alice using K1, read them, re-encrypt with K2, and forward to Bob. Neither party suspects the channel is compromised.
